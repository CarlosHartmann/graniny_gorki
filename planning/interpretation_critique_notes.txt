Top Findings (Ordered By Severity)

1. Your core claim for Hypothesis 2 relies on a post-hoc filtered human baseline that is not pre-specified and is highly decision-sensitive.
The interpretation uses culling plus top-half selection as the practical human comparator. This is understandable substantively, but inferentially it risks optimistic bias toward LLM superiority unless framed as exploratory/sensitivity analysis. Right now, the interpretation reads stronger than the design supports.

2. The Hypothesis 1 comparison is not symmetric across humans and LLMs.
In your top-10 ranking, humans are single participants but LLMs are averaged over runs. That shrinks LLM variance and can change rank ordering. Also, a synthetic human ensemble (majority vote) is included in the same pool, which is not comparable to an individual annotator. Your current interpretation should acknowledge this comparability issue.

3. Hypothesis 3 is currently incomplete, so your confirmatory narrative is structurally under-supported.
You announce H3 in the intro and then leave it unfinished in discussion. This creates a logic gap between stated aims and interpreted findings.

4. Group-comparison interpretation overreaches what the test structure currently demonstrates.
You compute three groups in table form, but the ANOVA comparison for significance is only between two groups. Then the prose infers broader conclusions on native/L2 and crowdworkers. The interpretation should be narrowed or tests expanded to match claims.

5. Qualitative claims are strong, but the qualitative method is not specified enough to validate them.
The qualitative section asserts themes and cognitive patterns, but there is no coding protocol, coder count, adjudication process, or quote selection logic. This weakens credibility of the most interesting human-vs-LLM interpretation.

6. The cost-effectiveness conclusion is plausible but currently one-sided and under-parameterized.
The conclusion treats cost-quality tradeoff as near-settled, while quality uncertainty, culling policy dependence, and error-type costs are not integrated quantitatively. A compact utility framing would make this much stronger.

7. The context narrative is promising, but currently split between intention and evidence.
You planned explicit logging of context access, and mention modified prompting in discussion, but the interpretation does not yet present concrete rates, precision/recall of context-needed detection, or examples where declared access changed outcomes.

8. Several unresolved placeholders currently break argumentative continuity.
You still have placeholders in core interpretive zones: literature framing, sourcing, theoretical definitions, H3 completion, and qualitative quantification. These are not cosmetic; they block interpretability of your strongest claims.

9. Methods reporting promises are not yet fully honored in prose.
You explicitly note justification for metric choice and mention MCC in methods notes, but the interpretive write-up does not yet give a crisp rationale and implication for readers.
